The Sociological Record

Western foundations and the distinct tradition of Indian sociology — thinkers, eras, and the core idea each one left behind. Built for UPSC Sociology Optional & IGNOU MA Sociology revision.

ERA 01 1830 – 1890s

The Founding Era

Sociology breaks from philosophy and theology, claiming society itself as an object that can be studied with the tools of science.

1798–1857 Founder

Auguste Comte

French

Core Contribution

Coined the term "sociology" and proposed positivism — the idea that society follows observable laws that can be studied scientifically, just like nature.

Positivism Social Statics/Dynamics UPSC: Paper I, Emergence of Sociology
1818–1883 Founder

Karl Marx

German

Core Contribution

Argued history is driven by class conflict rooted in who controls the means of production — the material basis of all social and political life.

Historical Materialism Class Conflict Alienation UPSC: Paper I, Unit 4(a)
1820–1903 Founder

Herbert Spencer

British

Core Contribution

Applied evolutionary theory to society, describing it as an organism that grows more complex and differentiated over time — "survival of the fittest" applied socially.

Social Evolutionism Organic Analogy UPSC: Paper I, Emergence of Sociology
ERA 02 1890s – 1920s

Classical Consolidation

The discipline gains its core methods and concepts — the "big three" whose frameworks still anchor most intro courses.

1858–1917 Classical

Émile Durkheim

French

Core Contribution

Established sociology as a rigorous discipline studying "social facts." Showed even private acts like suicide follow social patterns shaped by social integration.

Social Facts Anomie Functionalism UPSC: Paper I, Unit 4(b)
1864–1920 Classical

Max Weber

German

Core Contribution

Studied how culture and ideas (not just economics) shape society, and how modern life becomes dominated by rationalization and bureaucracy.

Verstehen Rationalization Ideal Types UPSC: Paper I, Unit 4(c)
1858–1918 Classical

Georg Simmel

German

Core Contribution

Pioneered micro-sociology, examining how forms of interaction (exchange, conflict, the stranger) shape everyday urban social life.

Social Forms The Metropolis IGNOU: MA Sociology core reading
ERA 03 1920s – 1960s

Mid-Century Structuralism

American sociology rises, building grand systems to explain how social order is maintained and how meaning is built through interaction.

1863–1931 Mid-Century

George Herbert Mead

American

Core Contribution

Founded symbolic interactionism — the self is formed through social interaction and the internalized perspective of others (the "generalized other").

Symbolic Interactionism The "I" and "Me" UPSC: Paper I, Unit 4(f)
1902–1979 Mid-Century

Talcott Parsons

American

Core Contribution

Built structural functionalism into a grand theory: society as a system of interdependent parts, each serving a function to maintain stability.

AGIL Schema Social System UPSC: Paper I, Unit 4(d)
1910–2003 Mid-Century

Robert K. Merton

American

Core Contribution

Refined functionalism with concepts like manifest vs. latent functions and strain theory, explaining deviance as a response to blocked goals.

Strain Theory Latent Function Self-Fulfilling Prophecy UPSC: Paper I, Unit 4(e)
ERA 04 1960s – 1980s

The Critical & Conflict Turn

A reaction against grand, stability-focused theory — attention shifts to power, performance, and the hidden structures behind everyday life.

1922–1982 Critical Turn

Erving Goffman

Canadian-American

Core Contribution

Developed dramaturgy — everyday life as a performance, with people managing "front stage" and "back stage" impressions.

Dramaturgy Impression Management IGNOU: MA Sociology core reading
1926–1984 Critical Turn

Michel Foucault

French

Core Contribution

Showed how power operates through knowledge and institutions (prisons, hospitals, schools), disciplining bodies without needing overt force.

Power/Knowledge Discipline Biopower IGNOU: MA Sociology core reading
1930–2002 Critical Turn

Pierre Bourdieu

French

Core Contribution

Explained how inequality reproduces itself through habitus and forms of capital (cultural, social) beyond just money.

Habitus Cultural Capital Field IGNOU: MA Sociology core reading
ERA 05 1980s – Present

Contemporary Sociology

Theory grapples with globalization, identity, and the blurred line between individual agency and social structure.

b. 1929 Contemporary

Jürgen Habermas

German

Core Contribution

Theorized the public sphere and "communicative action" — rational dialogue as the basis for democratic legitimacy, threatened by market and state power.

Public Sphere Communicative Action IGNOU: MA Sociology core reading
b. 1938 Contemporary

Anthony Giddens

British

Core Contribution

Developed structuration theory — structure and individual agency are not opposites but constantly produce each other through routine practice.

Structuration Reflexive Modernity IGNOU: MA Sociology core reading
b. 1956 Contemporary

Judith Butler

American

Core Contribution

Argued gender is performative — not an innate identity but something continually produced through repeated social acts and norms.

Performativity Gender Trouble IGNOU: MA Sociology core reading
ERA 01 1920s – 1950s

Colonial & Early Foundations

Indian sociology emerges from colonial-era ethnography and Indology, establishing the discipline's first institutional base at Bombay and Pune.

1893–1983 Founder

G. S. Ghurye

Indian · "Father of Indian Sociology"

Core Contribution

Founded the Indology approach — studied Indian society through Sanskritic texts and classical literature, treating caste as indigenous to Indian civilization rather than purely colonial. Established the Bombay school and trained generations of Indian sociologists.

Indology Caste & Race UPSC: Paper I, Unit 1(a)
1905–1970 Founder

Irawati Karve

Indian

Core Contribution

Pioneered the comparative study of Indian kinship systems, distinguishing the North Indian and South Indian patterns of marriage and descent. One of India's first trained women anthropologists.

Kinship Organisation North–South Kinship Divide UPSC: Paper II, Kinship
ERA 02 1950s – 1980s

Post-Independence Consolidation

Field-based empirical sociology takes hold; village studies, caste mobility, and Marxist critique become the discipline's central debates after Independence.

1916–1999 Post-Independence

M. N. Srinivas

Indian

Core Contribution

Shifted Indian sociology from "book view" to "field view" through village studies. Coined Sanskritization (lower castes adopting upper-caste customs to gain status) and the concept of the Dominant Caste.

Sanskritization Dominant Caste UPSC: Paper I, Unit 1(b)
1915–1994 Post-Independence

A. R. Desai

Indian

Core Contribution

Applied Marxist sociology to India — analyzed the social background of Indian nationalism through class and property relations, critiquing both colonial capitalism and post-colonial bourgeois democracy.

Marxist Sociology Social Background of Indian Nationalism UPSC: Paper I, Unit 1(c)
1911–1998 Post-Independence

Louis Dumont

French (Indologist)

Core Contribution

Proposed the purity–pollution framework for caste in Homo Hierarchicus, arguing caste is a unique system of hierarchy rooted in ritual status, not merely economic class.

Homo Hierarchicus Purity & Pollution UPSC: Paper II, Caste
ERA 03 1980s – Present

Contemporary Indian Sociology

Focus turns to modernization, social stratification, and the changing shape of caste and class in a globalizing India.

b. 1934 Contemporary

André Béteille

Indian

Core Contribution

Studied the interplay of caste, class, and power in Indian villages, distinguishing harmonic social structures (where caste, class, and power align) from disharmonic ones (where they diverge).

Harmonic / Disharmonic Structures Caste, Class & Power UPSC: Paper II, Caste
1932–2022 Contemporary

Yogendra Singh

Indian

Core Contribution

Theorized the modernization of Indian tradition — how Indian society absorbs modernity (rationality, universalism) while reshaping it through existing structures like caste and kinship rather than simply replacing them.

Modernization of Tradition Sanskritization vs. Westernization UPSC: Paper II, Social Change
